28.1 If the population frequency were known

Let’s now see how the exchangeability of an agent’s degrees of belief allows it to calculate probabilities about the units of a population. We shall do this calculation in two steps. First, in the case where the agent knows the joint frequency distribution (§§ 21.2, 21.3, 23.2) for the full population. Second, in the more general case where the agent lacks this population-frequency information.

When the full-population frequency distribution is known, the calculation of probabilities is very intuitive and analogous to the stereotypical “drawing balls from an urn”. We shall rely on this intuition; keep in mind, however, that the probabilities are not assigned “by intuition”, but actually fully determined by the two basic pieces of knowledge or assumptions: exchangeability and known population frequencies. Some simple proof sketches of this will also be given.

28.2 Notation

In this and the following chapters we shall stick to a particular use of some symbols, and shall often take the simplified income dataset (file income_nominal_data_all.csv and its underlying population as an example.

We consider an infinite population with any number of variates. For concreteness we assume these variates to have finite, discrete domains; but the formulae we obtain can be easily generalized to other kinds of variates. We shall denote all the population variates, jointly, with \({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z}\). In the case of the income dataset, for instance, the variate \({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z}\) stands for the joint variate with nine components:

\[ \begin{aligned} {\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z}&\coloneqq(\color[RGB]{68,119,170} \mathit{workclass} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} \mathit{education} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} \mathit{marital\_status} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} \mathit{occupation} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} {}\\ &\qquad \color[RGB]{68,119,170}\mathit{relationship} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} \mathit{race} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} \mathit{sex} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} \mathit{native\_country} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu} \mathit{income} \color[RGB]{0,0,0}) \end{aligned} \]

When we write \(\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z \mathord{\nonscript\mkern 1mu\textrm{\small=}\nonscript\mkern 1mu}\mathopen{}z\), the symbol \(\color[RGB]{68,119,170}z\) stands for some definite joint values, for instance \(({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}{\small\verb;Without-pay;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Doctorate;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\dotsb \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Ireland;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;>50K;}})\).

In applications typical of “supervised learning” (§  19), the agent wants to draw inferences about some of the \({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z}\) variates for a new unit, conditional on the values of the remaining variates. We call the joint variates to be inferred the predictand1, and we shall usually denote them jointly by the symbol \({\color[RGB]{238,102,119}Y}\). We call the variates used to inform the inference the predictor2, and we shall usually denote them jointly by the symbol \({\color[RGB]{34,136,51}X}\).

  • 1 literally “what has to be predicted”. In machine learning and other fields the terms “dependent variable”, “class” or “label” (for nominal variates) are often used.

  • 2 In machine learning and other fields the terms “independent variable” or “features” are often used

    • In the income problem, for instance, the agent (some USA census agency) would like to infer the \(\color[RGB]{238,102,119}\mathit{income}\) variate of a person from the other eight demographic characteristics \(\color[RGB]{34,136,51}\mathit{workclass} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\mathit{education} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\dotsb\) of that person. So in this inference problem we define

    \[ \begin{aligned} {\color[RGB]{238,102,119}Y}&\coloneqq{\color[RGB]{238,102,119}\mathit{income}} \\[1ex] {\color[RGB]{34,136,51}X}&\coloneqq({\color[RGB]{34,136,51}\mathit{workclass} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\mathit{education} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\dotsb \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\mathit{sex} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\mathit{native\_country}}) \end{aligned} \]

    We shall, however, also consider slightly different inference problems, for example with \(({\color[RGB]{238,102,119}\mathit{race} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\mathit{sex}})\) as predictand and the remaining seven variates \(({\color[RGB]{34,136,51}\mathit{workclass} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\dotsb \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}\mathit{income}})\) as predictors.

    28.3 Knowing the full-population frequency distribution

    Now suppose that the agent knows the full-population joint frequency distribution. Let’s make clearer what this means. In the income problem, for instance, consider these two different joint values for the joint variate \({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z}\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} {\color[RGB]{68,119,170}z'}&\coloneqq( {\small\verb;Private;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;HS-grad;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Married-civ-spouse;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Machine-op-inspct;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{} \\ &\qquad{\small\verb;Husband;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;White;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Male;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;United-States;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;<=50K;} ) \\[2ex] {\color[RGB]{68,119,170}z''}&\coloneqq( {\small\verb;Self-emp-not-inc;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;HS-grad;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Married-civ-spouse;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Farming-fishing;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{} \\ &\qquad{\small\verb;Husband;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;White;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;Male;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;United-States;} \mathbin{\mkern-0mu,\mkern-0mu}{\small\verb;<=50K;} ) \end{aligned} \]

    The agent knows that the value \(\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z\mathord{\nonscript\mkern 1mu\textrm{\small=}\nonscript\mkern 1mu}\mathopen{}z'\) occurs in the full population of interest (in this case all 340 millions or so USA citizens, considered in a short period of time) with a relative frequency \(0.860 369\%\); it also knows that the value \(\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z\mathord{\nonscript\mkern 1mu\textrm{\small=}\nonscript\mkern 1mu}\mathopen{}z''\) occurs with a relative frequency \(0.260 058\%\). We write this as follows:

    \[ f({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z\mathord{\nonscript\mkern 1mu\textrm{\small=}\nonscript\mkern 1mu}\mathopen{}z'}) = 0.860 369\% \ , \qquad f({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z\mathord{\nonscript\mkern 1mu\textrm{\small=}\nonscript\mkern 1mu}\mathopen{}z''}) = 0.260 058\% \]

    The agent knows not only the frequencies of the two particular joint values \(\color[RGB]{68,119,170}z'\), \(\color[RGB]{68,119,170}z''\), but for all possible joint values, that is, for all possible combinations of values from the single variate. In the income example there are 54 001 920 possible combinations, and therefore just as many relative frequencies. All these frequencies together form the full-population frequency distribution for \({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z}\), which we denote collectively with \(\boldsymbol{f}\) (note the boldface). This complete frequency information is denoted by the sentence \(\mathsfit{F}\).

    In other cases, these hypothetically known frequencies would refer to the full population of units: maybe even past, present, future, if they span a possibly unlimited time range.

    28.4 Inference about a single unit

    Now imagine that the agent must infer all \({\color[RGB]{68,119,170}Z}\) variates for a specific unit; in the income example, for a specific USA citizen. This unit could have one particular combination of variate values or another; in the income example it could have any one of the 54 001 920 possible combined values. The agent must assign a probability to each of these possibilities.3
